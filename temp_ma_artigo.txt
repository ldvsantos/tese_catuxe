Conservation agriculture practices are increasingly promoted to counter this trend, yet their cumulative impacts on the physical, chemical, and biological dimensions of soil quality remain insufficiently integrated. The objective of this study was to develop a functional soil quality framework for tropical agroecosystems by applying meta-analytic structural equation modeling (MASEM) to 230 paired comparisons from field studies published between 2015 and 2025. Effect sizes were calculated as log transformed response ratios (lnRR) within a robust mixed-effects modeling framework. Conservation practices particularly no-tillage, crop rotation, and organic residue management were consistently associated with improvements in soil properties, including increases in soil organic carbon (+15.5%), water-stable aggregates (+26.0%), and macroporosity (+20.6%). The structural model revealed that physical attributes exerted the strongest direct influence on soil quality (β = 0.701; p < 0.001), followed by chemical (β = 0.438; p = 0.004) and biological factors (β = 0.200; p = 0.036). Moreover, a significant interaction between physical and chemical dimensions (β = 0.309) underscores the interdependence of soil structure and nutrient dynamics under tropical conditions. These results move beyond previous syntheses by providing causal evidence that conservation agriculture enhances soil resilience through synergistic pathways, directly linking soil functions to the delivery of ecosystem services. This integrative framework offers robust quantitative support for policies designed to scale sustainable land management and mitigate soil degradation in tropical agroecosystems under intensifying climatic pressures.Keywords: soil conservation | quantitative meta-analysis | conservation tillage | functional soil indicators | agroecosystem services | tropical agroecosystems | structural equation modeling1 IntroductionIntensive agricultural systems, especially those reliant on conventional tillage, are major drivers of soil degradation in tropical regions. Repeated ploughing and low organic inputs accelerate the mineralization of soil organic matter, induce compaction, reduce microbial diversity, and weaken nutrient cycling. These processes hinder aggregate formation, deplete soil carbon stocks, and compromise long-term agroecosystem sustainability (Ahngar et al. 2020; Dassou et al. 2024; Estevam et al. 2021). Although tillage may temporarily improve aeration and root penetration, it exposes topsoil to erosion, disrupts soil fauna, and accelerates organic matter losses, leading to nutrient depletion and reduced resilience (Jiang et al. 2025; Morais et al. 2025).In contrast, conservation agriculture (CA) practices—including no-tillage, cover cropping, crop rotation, and residue management—are increasingly promoted to restore soil health. These practices enhance structure, water retention, microbial activity, and carbon sequestration, thereby sustaining productivity (Grandinetti et al. 2022; Ács et al. 2024). Evidence shows that CA improves diverse indicators, from aggregate stability and infiltration to microbial biomass and labile carbon fractions (Schlüter et al. 2022; Sifola et al. 2024). Their urgency is underscored by projections that over 90% of arable land could face severe degradation by 2050 without effective interventions, with South America, Southeast Asia, and Sub-Saharan Africa already among the most affected regions (Borrelli et al. 2017; Panagos et al. 2020).Despite this evidence, most syntheses evaluate soil indicators in isolation, overlooking interactions among physical, chemical, and biological dimensions. Variability across soils, climates, and management systems further complicates the formulation of robust, generalizable strategies (Abdallah et al. 2021; Pečan et al. 2023). There is a pressing need for integrative frameworks that capture cumulative and interactive effects, linking soil processes to ecosystem service delivery and informing sustainable land management in tropical agroecosystems (Francaviglia et al. 2023; Suleiman 2024).Here, we test the hypothesis that conservation practices generate measurable improvements in integrated soil quality. We apply meta-analytic structural equation modeling (MASEM) to a comprehensive dataset of field studies, providing a holistic assessment of how CA influences multiple soil attributes and enhances resilience under global change.2 Materials and MethodsThis study followed PRISMA 2020 guidelines for systematic reviews and meta-analyses. Guided by the hypothesis that conservation practices improve integrated soil quality, we developed a conceptual framework to organize variables and outcomes.To move beyond conventional syntheses, we applied meta-analytic structural equation modeling (MASEM) to quantify causal pathways among soil indicators. Soil quality was modeled as a construct with three functional dimensions—physical, chemical, and biological—hypothesized to interact synergistically and to be directly influenced by nature-based practices.The practices considered included no-tillage, mulching, crop rotation, and structural interventions (e.g., terraces, geotextiles, palisades, infiltration basins). Each was coded according to its expected contribution to soil functions and linked to specific quality indicators, providing the basis for data extraction, classification, and moderator selection.Eligibility criteria were defined using the PICOS framework (Population, Intervention, Comparator, Outcomes, Study design) to ensure transparency and comparability. This approach strengthened methodological traceability and supported integration of diverse evidence into a single functional assessment of soil quality.TABLE 1 PICOS framework defining the eligibility criteria for studies included in the meta-analysis.ElementDescriptionP (Population)Field studies conducted in tropical and subtropical agroecosystems, evaluating the effects of soil management on physical, chemical, and biological attributes of soil quality.I (Intervention)Conservation agriculture practices, including no-tillage, minimum tillage, mulching, crop rotation, subsoiling, cover crops, organic residue management, and green manures.C (Comparison)Conventional agricultural systems, such as ploughing and harrowing, monocultures, or management without conservation practices.O (Outcomes)Soil quality indicators representing physical (bulk density, porosity, water-stable aggregates, infiltration, hydraulic conductivity), chemical (SOC, TN, pH, CEC, labile and stable organic fractions), and biological (microbial biomass, enzymatic activity, macrofauna abundance) dimensions.S (Study design)Comparative field experiments (treatment vs. control) published between 2015 and 2025, reporting quantitative data (means, sample sizes, and measures of variance).2.1 Automated Procedure for Screening and Categorizing StudiesTo ensure transparency and reproducibility, we followed PRISMA 2020 standards for study selection. Bibliographic records were retrieved from Scopus, Web of Science, and the CAPES Theses and Dissertations Portal, covering both peer-reviewed and regional literature on tropical agroecosystems. Records were exported in BibTeX, merged into a unified database, and duplicates removed.Titles, abstracts, and metadata were screened against three criteria: (i) evaluation of conservation-oriented soil management practices; (ii) quantitative assessment of physical, chemical, or biological soil indicators (including organic fractions and vegetation-related metrics); and (iii) field-based experimental designs with comparative treatments.Studies were assigned to mutually exclusive functional categories (e.g., geotechnical, biological, physical, chemical, organic) to ensure consistent classification within the soil quality framework. Reviews, book chapters, and non-English publications were excluded.The final curated dataset included study metadata (region, practice, indicators, year, DOI) and was validated through automated routines and expert cross-checking, minimizing redundancy and ensuring full traceability for the meta-analysis.2.2 Eligibility Criteria and Data ExtractionAfter duplicate removal, studies were retained if they met the following criteria: (i) empirical, field-based designs (experimental, quasi-experimental, or controlled observational) directly comparing conservation and conventional soil management; (ii) quantitative data for physical or chemical attributes such as soil organic carbon, pH, bulk density, or hydraulic conductivity, with mean values and number of replicates, and measures of variance (SE or SD); (iii) accessible data in text, tables, or figures, with graphical information digitized using WebPlotDigitizer v4.3 (Rohatgi 2020); and (iv) comparisons applied within the same experimental site to control for edaphic and climatic variability, with consistent conservation techniques across treatments. Studies using residues with toxic or contaminating potential (e.g., sewage sludge) were excluded.2.3 Treatment ClassificationTreatments were categorized by three operational attributes: tillage depth, intensity of mechanical disturbance, and surface residue cover. This standardized framework (Table 2) ensured consistent comparison of conservation and conventional systems across tropical field studies. Missing values were estimated using multiple imputation by chained equations (MICE; van Buuren & Groothuis-Oudshoorn, 2011), with CART applied as the univariate method to generate 20 datasets. Final estimates were pooled following Rubin’s rules (Rubin, 1987). The database was structured hierarchically to account for dependencies:Level 1 (observation): each entry represented a paired comparison between conservation and control, linked to a specific variable (e.g., SOC, BD, Ks), depth, and unit, with lnRR and variance calculated.Level 2 (study): grouped observations from the same source, with random effects capturing shared edaphic and methodological conditions.Level 3 (meta-analysis): mixed-effects meta-regressions incorporated fixed moderators (management, depth, soil texture) and nested random effects.This design maximized information use, minimized bias, and ensured robust detection of both general and context-specific responses.2.6 Statistical AnalysisTo understand the effects of soil conservation management techniques, the logarithm of the response ratio (lnRR) was adopted as the effect size metric, calculated as:𝑙𝑛𝑅𝑅=𝑙𝑛𝑋𝑡𝑟𝑒𝑎𝑡𝑚𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑋𝑐𝑜𝑛𝑡𝑟𝑜𝑙(1)where 𝑋𝑡𝑟𝑒𝑎𝑡𝑚𝑒𝑛𝑡  treatment represents the average of the soil functional indicator in the conservation treatment and 𝑋𝑐𝑜𝑛𝑡𝑟𝑜𝑙  is the mean under the conventional system.The lnRR values > 0 indicate an increase in the functional indicator under conservation management, while lnRR values < 0 indicate a reduction, log values can also be understood as a percentage (%) of the difference in means among treatments. Analyses were performed on standardized data (n = 80) using the plspm package in R, with centroid weighting (Mastana 2025).The model specified three first-order latent constructs: Physical (EMACRO, PR, PAWC, BD, WSA, Q), Chemical (AMg, SOC, pH, TN, ATP, AK), and Biological (BR, MBC, qMic, qCO₂). These were combined into a second-order latent variable, the Functional Indicator, using the repeated-indicators approach.Model evaluation included composite reliability (DG.rho > 0.70), average variance extracted (AVE ≥ 0.50), discriminant validity (HTMT < 0.90), collinearity (VIF < 5), and global fit (SRMR < 0.08). R², indicator loadings (λ), and weights were examined, and results were visualized in a path diagram to interpret functional links between soil attributes and agroecosystem resilience. 3 Results Cochran’s Q test indicated significant heterogeneity among effect sizes (Q(5) = 72.94; p < 0.001). Among individual attributes, available phosphorus (ATP) showed the strongest response (lnRR = 0.253; +29%), followed by potassium (AK; lnRR = 0.238; +27%), magnesium (AMg; lnRR = 0.236; +27%), and soil organic carbon (SOC; lnRR = 0.149; +16%). Conservation practices reduced erosion rates by 6.7% (lnRR = –0.069; p < 0.001) and surface runoff by more than 40% (lnRR = –0.517; p < 0.001). Reduced runoff further indicates greater rainfall interception and infiltration, reinforcing the broader ecosystem service benefits of conservation-oriented systems (Singh et al. 2023).3.2 Biological VariablesMeta-analysis of biological attributes indicated that conservation practices generally enhanced microbial activity and community functioning. The overall effect was marginally significant (lnRR = 0.253; 95% CI = –0.010–0.516; p = 0.059), representing ~29% higher values under sustainable management, highlighting a consistent positive trend.Among individual variables, basal respiration (BR) increased strongly (lnRR = 0.333; 95% CI = 0.245–0.422; p < 0.001), equivalent to ~40% gain. This reflects greater microbial metabolic activity and improved substrate availability under residue retention and reduced disturbance. Combined with increases in microbial biomass and carbon-use efficiency, these results demonstrate that conservation practices not only enhance soil fertility but also strengthen the functional structure and resilience of microbial communities.3.3 Effects on Sustainable Soil ManagementAgroforestry and organic systems produced the strongest improvements in soil functionality (lnRR = 0.275; p < 0.001). These practices combine plant diversity, organic inputs, and the absence of synthetic chemicals, enhancing nutrient cycling, structural stability, and biological activity. In agroforestry, perennial species foster stable microclimates, greater litter inputs, and deeper organic enrichment, contributing to carbon sequestration and erosion control (Fahad et al. 2022; Sharma et al. 2025).Organic systems reinforced biotic processes through continuous organic amendments and minimal tillage, supporting microbial activity and nutrient mineralization, with higher plant–microbial diversity translating into greater multifunctionality and resilience under climatic stressors (Singh et al. 2024; Souza et al. 2025; Huera-Lucero et al. 2025).No-tillage also showed significant positive effects (lnRR = 0.088; p = 0.022), consistent with evidence that reduced disturbance promotes organic matter accumulation, aggregate stability, and improved moisture and nutrient retention (Omer et al. 2024; Singh et al. 2023).TABLE 4 Multivariate meta-regression results for the average effects of different soil management categories. This distinction underscores that nutrient availability often operates indirectly through soil structure and microbial processes./FIGURE 3 Variable Importance in Projection (VIP) scores for predictor variables in the soil functional quality model, estimated using Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modeling (PLS-SEM). Stable aggregates protect organic matter, enhance nutrient retention, and are stabilized further by organo–mineral complexes, increasing cation exchange capacity for key nutrients such as Mg²⁺, K⁺, and bioavailable P (Bronick & Lal 2005). Our meta-analysis confirmed these mechanisms, with significant increases in ATP (+29%), AK (+27%), and AMg (+27%). Unlike previous studies that evaluated single fractions in isolation (Rahmati et al. 2020; Liu et al. 2023), our structural modeling demonstrates that these chemical gains are largely mediated by aggregation and microbial processes, underscoring the systemic nature of fertility improvements.These chemical responses were strongly coupled with physical and hydrological benefits. Conservation practices increased SOC (+16%), water-stable aggregates (+55.6%), and plant-available water capacity (+20.4%), while reducing bulk density (–7.7%), erosion, and runoff (>40%). Such shifts enhance porosity, infiltration, and resilience against extreme rainfall, confirming that hydrological regulation is a fundamental pathway through which conservation agriculture sustains multifunctionality (Komissarov & Klik 2020; Silva et al. 2021; Singh et al. 2023).Biological responses reinforced these outcomes. Residue inputs and reduced disturbance stimulated microbial biomass carbon (+52.1%), microbial quotient (+36.6%), and efficiency (lower qCO₂). Although the structural model attributed a smaller direct effect to biology (β = 0.200), the meta-analysis showed consistently positive responses, consistent with findings from agroforestry and organic systems (Huera-Lucero et al. 2025; Luan et al. 2020). These results confirm that microbial gains emerge as systemic properties mediated by chemical and physical improvements.VIP analysis identified SOC, AMg, PAWC, MBC, and qMic as sentinel indicators (VIP > 1), suitable for large-scale monitoring of conservation outcomes. Their prioritization can improve the scalability and cost-effectiveness of soil restoration programs, providing actionable guidance for policy and management.Comparisons among systems showed that no-tillage and legume rotations promoted organic matter accumulation and aggregation, while straw management and residue cover were critical for microclimate regulation and erosion control (Li et al. 2025; Sartori et al. 2022). This highlights that while reduced tillage matters, sustained residue retention is the key driver of long-term soil resilience.From a broader perspective, our findings align with global syntheses documenting the effectiveness of conservation agriculture (Blanco-Canqui et al. 2024; Munna & Lal 2025), but extend them by providing causal evidence of synergistic pathways. By explicitly linking nutrient dynamics, structure, and biology, this study demonstrates that soil resilience in tropical agroecosystems emerges from integrated processes, addressing long-standing critiques of fragmented indicator assessments.5 ConclusionThis study shows that soil functional quality emerges from synergistic interactions among physical, chemical, and biological attributes, reinforcing the systemic nature of soil functioning. Conservation and agroecological practices proved highly effective in promoting soil multifunctionality.Physical structure was the strongest driver, confirming the central role of aggregation and porosity. Chemical composition also contributed but was largely dependent on stable soil matrices, demonstrating that nutrient availability alone is insufficient. Biological attributes responded positively to increased biodiversity and reduced disturbance, underscoring their sensitivity and value as functional indicators.The importance of indirect pathways—particularly the mediating role of physical properties in nutrient dynamics and microbial processes—highlights the complexity of soil interactions. At a broader scale, conservation practices enhanced multiple ecosystem functions simultaneously: improving nutrient availability, stabilizing structure, regulating hydrology, and supporting efficient microbial communities.Together, these findings provide empirical evidence that sustainable management can accelerate soil recovery, restore ecological functions, and maintain productivity in vulnerable tropical landscapes.ReferencesAbdallah, A.
